Community Experience Professionalism

The high school experience at St. Benedict's is a time for personal growth and change; this can be both exhilarating and very challenging. These challenges can be stressful and interfere with your son's academic, social, or emotional growth. If you recognize these struggles, we invite you to schedule a confidential appointment and discuss your concerns with one of our counselors in a safe, caring environment.

We also encourage parents, guardians, or the students themselves to self-report concerns before they become out of control. St. Benedict's Prep is a college preparatory school. At the end of the day, our primary mission is to educated young men, while developing their skills for future advancement. Anything that interferes with or discourages your son's academic/ interpersonal growth is a problem. Do not hesistate to reach out to us for help. Make an appointment. That is why we are here.

about

The formal guidance staff includes the Head of Guidance, a social worker and school psychologist. This office coordinates crisis intervention, testing for various learning deficiencies, as well as group and individual counseling for various issues (substance abuse, how to cope with a dysfunctional family situation, etc.). Referrals are made through this office to outside agencies.

Because a relatively close and on-going relationship with a concerned adult can often make a significant difference in a young man's life, much of the counseling at Saint Benedict's is often informal. Teachers and administrators speak with students in a variety of contexts, including the daily group period, and can refer serious or persistent difficulties to the Guidance Office.

counseling services

  • Individual

    Students seek individual counseling for a variety of reasons. Providing a safe and respectful atmosphere where students have an opportunity to explore their thoughts, feelings, behaviors, and experiences of themselves and others in their lives can positively impact a student’s academic experience. Individual counseling gives a student a place to organize their thoughts and learn new skills and ways of thinking that may benefit them throughout life.

    Our goal is to empower students with the ability to effectively solve problems on their own. Some of the concerns that students have sought therapy for include: Anger, Depression, Anxiety, Relationship concerns, Academic stress, Time management, Trauma, Family conflict, Identity concerns, Sexual concerns, LGBT concerns, Social skills].

    If you believe counseling may be helpful, or if you have questions about our services, please stop by the counseling center.

  • Group

    Our Counseling Center has offered a variety of groups over the years. Groups are a helpful way to address concerns while gaining the support of your peers. Group counseling involves a counselor working with several students who share the same experiences in a safe, supportive environment. Groups may be integrated as part of a comprehensive treatment plan along with individual therapy and/or medication management. Groups offer students the opportunity for validation, peer understanding and support, interpersonal learning, hope, information sharing, and building up of self-confidence and self-esteem. Groups may be ongoing or time limited.

    We currently offer the following groups:

    Unknown Sons Group - An ongoing, skill building support group for students and mothers to collaborate, share experiences and ideas, and learn healthy ways of coping with the various stressors and responsibilities that come with the dual role of parent and student. Meetings are held twice a week at the Counseling Center.

    Blue Man Group - A therapeutic support group that provides a safe environment to share experiences, find acceptance, and validation. This group will allow members to address such topics as trauma, emotions, self-esteem, coping mechanisms, relationships and trust. This group is short term over eight weekly sessions. Meetings are held at the Counseling Center.

    Anger Management Group – This skill based group focuses on building effective social interaction skills. The group focuses on learning.

    Ala-Teen - If there is a group you would like to see offered at the Counseling Center, let us know. If you are interested in attending one of the above groups, come down to the Counseling Center to sign up.

  • Family

    Relationships play a central role in all of our lives, but they obviously take a lot of work. The issues that face each family are complex, unique, and are not easily improved by utilizing some pre-packaged formula. We encourage individuals and families to take an honest look at themselves as the launching point for change.

    Treatment may also include learning more effective interpersonal skills, learning new ways to cope with stress and frustration, or addressing dynamics that cause emotional pain and disappointment.

    Parents may request to work with a counselor individually, with their spouse, or with the entire family to seek a new understanding of the problems they are facing and develop a new means of coping with it. This exploration often lasts from five to ten sessions of fifty minutes each but may continue over a more extended period of time.

  • Referrals

    At the Counseling Center, you will be expected to provide detailed medical records describing your son's diagnosis and past treatment. Corroborative information, such as a parent/guardian reports, academic records, including any history of accommodations, formal assessment data, standardized questionnaires, etc. may be required.

    Our staff will review this assembled data to create an appropriate treatment plan for your son. If our diagnostic interviewing proves inconclusive, or if the situation demands more treatment than we can provide, additional neuropsychological or psychoeducational testing may be requested. We will guide you in acquiring the appropriate treatment at a center off the property.

Psychiatric Services

Our team of mental health professionals includes psychiatrists on staff who are available to evaluate each patient's special needs and to discuss medication and therapy options with the patients. Psychiatrists can recommend and prescribe medications that are effective for depression, bipolar disorder, panic disorder, obsessive compulsive disorder and for other mental illnesses. If the patient is prescribed medication, the psychiatrist will monitor the case and provide medication management. Please note students are responsible for the costs of filling their prescriptions.

ADHD/ADD Evaluation &Treatment

Problems with attention and focus can adversely affect academic and social functioning resulting in significant stress for college students. The Counseling Center staff is available to provide psychological for Attention Deficit Disorder and Attention Deficit and Hyperactivity Disorder (ADD/ADHD). The Counseling staff also assist to provide evaluations for ADD/ADHD and assist with referral.

Crisis Intervention & Emergencies

For immediate assistance during a crisis, please call Mr. Rowe, Mr. Scanlan or Dr. Lamourt or 911 if you are off Abbey grounds. If you are experiencing a psychological crisis and would like to speak with someone, please come to the Counseling Center during office hours to speak with a clinician. After-hours, please call the front desk or visit your local Emergency Room.

If you are faculty, staff, or a student and have serious concerns about the mental health or safety of a student, please call the Counseling Center for a consultation. To report a concern, please contact Mr. Rowe, Mr. Scanlan or Dr. Lamourt.

If you are a student who has experienced a trauma and are in crisis, The Counseling Center will receive walk-in visits from students who are experiencing emotional distress in response to traumatic events. Resources

  • Faculty & Staff Resources

    Within our community, the Faculty & Staff are more often than not the first people to recognize that a student is experiencing some kind of difficulty. Recognizing these situations can be overwhelming as you may not be sure of what to do or how to best help your student, his peers, or yourself. We hope that the following information will assist you by providing a sense of clarity and support.

    • Assisting Students

      During these difficult situations, there is no need to feel as though you must investigate the situation, diagnose a student, or provide counseling. Your role in this kind of situation is to follow the guidelines that have been implemented by the faculty hand book. Your contribution helps the Committee come together to provide the student, yourself, and the campus community the help and safety needed to address and remedy the concerning situation. Crises are often the result of distress brewing over time. Early intervention can be the key to addressing a student’s needs as soon as possible. This makes your contribution to the process vital and much appreciated.

    • Threat Assessment & Safety Protocol [TASP]

      The Threat Assessment & Safety Protocol at St. Benedict's utilizes four (4) metrics to help faculty and staff members address certain student behaviors. These four (4) metrics are provided as a general guide; please remember that all possible situations my not fit neatly into these categories. If you feel uncomfortable or are confused about what steps to take after encountering a particular situation, please contact the Director of the Counseling Department [Dr. Ivan Lamourt], and or the Dean of Discipline [Mr. John Rowe] by phone or via email.

    • Identifying Concenring Bahaviors

      1.) Distressed

      Distressed behavior from a student may cause you to be concerned about the student's personal well-being. Examples of Distressed Behavior: Crying, highly agitated, anvious, panicking, shutting down academically/ socially.

      2.) Disturbed

      Disturbing behavior from a student may cause you personal frustration as a teacher. Student's exhibiting disturbing behavior may be highly inappropriate, irrational, delusional, and or make other students feel uncomfortable. Examples of Disturbed Behavior: Extreme anger/ agitation, paranoia, hallucinations, disorganized thought/ speech.

      3.) Disruptive

      Disruptive behavior from a student will obviously cause problem for you in the classroom. Disruptive behavior can be defined as any escalation, "acting out", or inner disturbance that inteferes with others. Examples of Disruptive Behavior: Hostile/ defiant attitudes, harassing/ bullying others, monopolizing, and or chronically violating rules.

      4.) Dangerous

      Dangerous behavior from a student can be categorized as any behavior that threatens the safety or well-being of others. Examples of Dangerous Behavior: Threats (verbal, written, web-based), substance abuse, frequent unprovoked physical altercations.

    • Contacting the Department

      Accurate records are crtitical in improving our ability to respond appropriately to situations with students as they arise. Please contact the Director of Counseling (Dr. Ivan Lamourt) immediately if you believe the situation warrants concern. Trust you judgement.

  • Parent & Guardian Resources

    Parents and families can experience many feelings while their children attend St. Benedict's. The many milestones, along the way to graduation, may bring up pride and joy, as well as other personal concerns. Some parents may worry that their child is not ready for these challenges. Others may be concerned about the potential impact of increased stress from academic and athletic expectations.

    Regardless of the situation, we would like to reassure you that there are a number of resources within the school that are available to all of our students. The Counseling Center is just one place that can help provide your child (or family) with the mental health support that you need.

    • Signs of Distress

      As a parent, you may notice changes in your son’s behavior from time to time. If perhaps you are not sure of the cause, these are some behaviors to look out for [ which may be an indication that your child is in need of some assistance]:

      Not attending class
      Disproportionate responses to grades
      Hostility/anger outbursts
      Being more withdrawn
      Easily irritated
      Pacing, jitteriness
      Noticeable marks (e.g., cuts, bruises)
      Visible changes in weight
      Deterioration of physical appearance
      Being a recent victim of violence or other traumatic event
      Significant sadness or sense of hopelessness

    • Counseling Services

      The Counseling Center offers many different services including:
      Individual (Family Counseling)
      Psychiatric Medication Management
      Referrals to Off-the-Property Treatment
      Workshops/Consultation

    • Confidentiality

      We are committed and mandated to maintain confidentiality of all of our students. Outside of instances of imminent risk, we protect the privacy of our students and work hard to make the Counseling Center a safe place for our students. While these regulations are in the best interest of your son it can be challenging for parents. You may want to ask specific information about your son only to be advised that we cannot reveal any information, including their attendance. This does not mean however that we do not value the role of family in our student’s lives and treatment. Parents can consult with us on general matters or to share concerns and ask questions about how to help their child in the event that they are noticing peculiar behavior. More specific communication would require a release of information signed by your son.

  • Crisis & Emergencies

    Students who are experiencing a psychological or emotional crisis often need immediate help and intervention. Some examples of serious crises include:

    - Suicidal or homicidal thoughts/ impulses
    - Sexual or physical assault
    - Hearing voices or otherwise misperceiving reality
    - Overwhelming loss, such as a death in the family
    - Not having their own food, clothing or shelter
    - Having a severe reaction to psychiatric medication

    If any of the above apply to your current situation, it is appropriate for you to have or recommend a crisis session. Contact a staff member from the Guidance Department immediately.

    • Not a crisis?

      If none of these situations apply, but you still believe that the Guidance Department needs to be notified, please contact us immediately.

      [N.B. Faculty & Staff] Remember, you do not have to teach or coach within an unacceptable learning environment. Communicating your concern to your student can serve as a “teachable moment.” You should consider if communication should occur before, during, or after class. Faculty members may ask a disruptive student to leave the class once. Disruptive behavior beyond that should immediatley be reported to a parent/ guardian, Mr. Rowe, and a member of the Guidance Department.

    • Emergencies

      If a student does not respond to your initial attempt to de-escalate situation, report the incident to Mr. Rowe immediately. In these situations, Mr. Rowe will determine the appropriateness of a referral to the Guidance Department.

  • Frequently Asked Questions

    Students (and Families) come to the Guidance Department with a wide range of concerns. Some are dealing with a diagnosis of depression, anxiety, bipolar disorder, or post-traumatic stress disorder. Others do not have a mental health diagnosis but feel like they need additional support in dealing with the challenges of life. Counseling can provide a safe environment to discuss the issues you are dealing with and help you find healthy ways to cope.

    • How do I make an appointment?

      Come visit Guidance Department, located within the Radel Building at St. Benedict’s, to begin the process.

    • How much does counseling cost?

      Counseling is free to all members (students and faculty) of the St. Benedict's prep community. Families and other parties will be billed through their insurance provider.

    • Can I come for someone else?

      Yes. Sometimes students have serious concerns about their peers. Our staff will talk to students about how to deal with and help their friends. Still, all information about other clients will be held in strict confidence.

    • Will what I discuss be kept confidential?

      Any information shared during counseling sessions, in addition to the fact that the student has been seen for counseling, is kept strictly confidential. Disclosure to any outside person, including friends or family, requires the prior written permission of the student. However, confidentiality will be broken if the clinician thinks you are a threat to yourself or someone else, if you disclose information about child or elder abuse, or if legal proceedings require disclosure of your counseling sessions, in adherence with state licensing laws.

    • Will receiving counseling go on my personal record?

      No, absolutely not. Guidance Department records are not part of the school's official student records.

    • Do I continue with the clinician who I see first?

      Not always. Not all problems are appropriate for treatment here at the Guidance Department. So after the first meeting, a clinician will determine how to best meet your individual needs. A student may be referred to another counselor off campus or to other specialists if that is the appropriate course of action. More often than not, our students remain within the Guidance Department. However, a clinician may refer a student to another member of the Guidance Department as well.

    • Is counseling mandated?

      Our services are voluntary; however, judicial, academic or housing administrators may mandate counseling as part of some disciplinary action.

  • Internships

    Field learning is a focal point of any counselor education program. We welcome counselors in training that need the opportunity to combine their didactic learning with actual experience in an educational environment. By interning within our guidance department, you will be able to observe, develop clinical skills, and practice the tasks typically associated with a professional counselor.

    • How to apply?

      Please contact the Director of Counseling, Dr. Ivan Lamourt, via email with a brief explanation of your interneship needs. In the past, we have accomodated several students from across the tri-state are in fulfilling their internship requirements.

    • Required Course Work

      Students are expected to have successfully completed at least the following courses prior to beginning their internship: Professional Orientation and Ethical Issues in Counseling, Foundations of Counseling, Cross-Cultural Counseling, Counseling: Theory and Process, Individual Counseling: Practice (Lab) I and II, Abnormal Psychology, and Group Dynamics.
